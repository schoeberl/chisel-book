# Tutorial Proposal: Agile Hardware Design with Chisel

## Title

**Agile Hardware Design with Chisel**

## Organizer and speaker

**Prof. Martin Schoeberl, Technical University of Denmark**

Martin Schoeberl is a Professor at the Technical University of Denmark. He received his PhD from the Vienna University of Technology in 2005 and was an Assistant Professor at the Institute of Computer Engineering from 2005 to 2010. His research interests include hard real-time systems, time-predictable computer architecture, real-time Java, digital design, and open-source hardware. He has been involved in several national and international research projects, including JEOPARD, CJ4ES, T-CREST, RTEMP, the TACLe COST action, and PREDICT, and was the technical lead of the EC-funded T-CREST project. He has more than 200 peer-reviewed publications.

Martin has worked closely with the Chisel community during several research stays at UC Berkeley and led the T-CREST project, where most components were written in Chisel. He is the author of the open-source book **Digital Design with Chisel**: https://github.com/schoeberl/chisel-book. The book is used at several universities and will serve as background material for this tutorial.

## Proposed format and length

**Half-day tutorial**, approximately 4 hours including breaks.

The tutorial combines short lectures, live coding, and hands-on generator development. It uses Chisel as the implementation language, but the main focus is agile hardware-generator methodology: how to move from a fixed hardware component to a configurable generator, how to test generated instances, and how to use parameters for design-space exploration.

The tutorial is based on material from a full-semester DTU course on agile hardware design with Chisel: https://github.com/schoeberl/agile-hw. For FPL, this material will be condensed into a focused half-day format emphasizing reusable, testable, and evolvable hardware generators.

## Abstract

Modern FPGA and accelerator research increasingly depends on hardware generators rather than single fixed RTL blocks. A generator captures a family of related designs, for example different data widths, buffer depths, pipeline configurations, memory sizes, or interconnect structures. This style supports reuse and design-space exploration, but generators must be engineered carefully to avoid fragile meta-programming and untested parameter combinations.

This tutorial presents an agile approach to hardware-generator development using Chisel. Participants will learn how to start from a minimal working design, introduce parameters incrementally, refactor repeated structure into reusable components, and write tests for multiple generated configurations. The necessary Chisel concepts are introduced as tools for generator construction; the tutorial is not intended as a general Chisel syntax course.

The tutorial emphasizes small design increments, executable tests, generator-level validation, refactoring, continuous integration, and design-space exploration. Example topics include configurable FIFOs, parameterized streaming pipelines, small accelerator skeletons, and ready/valid components. Participants will be supported by the open-source book **Digital Design with Chisel** and by material derived from the DTU agile hardware design course.

## Motivation and relevance to FPL

FPGA and reconfigurable-computing research often requires rapid construction of design variants. Researchers may need to evaluate different pipeline depths, memory organizations, arithmetic widths, accelerator configurations, or interconnect structures. Writing each variant manually in RTL is error-prone and limits exploration.

This tutorial addresses a central productivity problem in FPGA-based research: how to construct reusable and configurable hardware without losing confidence in correctness. Chisel provides a suitable environment because it supports abstraction, parameterization, type checking, and software-style testing. The tutorial connects directly to FPL themes such as reusable accelerator infrastructure, design-space exploration, open-source hardware, and education-oriented hardware construction.

The intended outcome is that participants leave with a concrete workflow for developing hardware generators incrementally, testing them systematically, and integrating them into larger FPGA or ASIC-oriented projects.

## Existing material and maturity

The tutorial is not a one-off experiment. It builds on a full-semester DTU course on agile hardware design with Chisel and on the open-source book **Digital Design with Chisel**. This provides a mature base of lectures, examples, and exercises. The FPL tutorial selects the parts most relevant to the conference community: hardware generators, testing across generated configurations, agile iteration, refactoring, and design-space exploration.

## Intended audience

The tutorial is aimed at participants who know basic digital design and want to move beyond hand-written RTL modules toward reusable hardware generators. The audience includes FPGA researchers, accelerator designers, PhD students, educators, and engineers. Verilog or VHDL experience is sufficient; prior Chisel experience is useful but not required.

## Learning objectives

After the tutorial, participants should be able to:

1. Explain why hardware generators are useful for FPGA and accelerator research.
2. Develop a generator incrementally from a fixed design.
3. Design clear configuration interfaces for hardware generators.
4. Write tests that cover multiple generated hardware instances.
5. Use generator parameters for design-space exploration.
6. Avoid common generator-design pitfalls such as untested parameter combinations and over-generalization.
7. Integrate generator-based development into an agile hardware workflow.

## Hands-on examples

The recommended running example is a configurable FIFO or streaming pipeline. It is small enough for a half-day tutorial but rich enough to discuss generator interfaces, testing, corner cases, and design-space exploration. Optional examples include a small accelerator skeleton or a configurable interconnect fragment.

## Tutorial material and requirements

The tutorial will provide slides derived from the DTU course, a small starter repository with Chisel generator examples, step-by-step exercises, simulation-based tests, and pointers to **Digital Design with Chisel**. The exercises can be run locally; a Docker or devcontainer setup can be provided to reduce installation issues.

A standard tutorial room with projector and internet access is sufficient. Participants should bring a laptop. No FPGA board is required.
